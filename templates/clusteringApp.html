01 — Data Context

What this dataset represents

This dataset is a marketing campaign response record from a retail company. Each row represents one customer and captures their demographic profile, spending across product categories, and whether they responded to previous marketing campaigns.

The business goal is to segment customers into distinct groups based on income and spending habits — enabling future campaigns to be targeted to each segment, maximising return on ad spend and improving customer retention. K-Means is applied as an unsupervised technique: the model discovers natural groupings in the data without any pre-defined labels, revealing archetypes from budget-conscious shoppers to high-value loyalists.

03 — Data Preprocessing

01

Missing Value Removal

The Income column contained {{ data.nulls_dropped }} null values (~{{ "%.1f" | format(data.nulls_dropped / data.total_raw * 100) }}% of records). Rows with missing income were dropped — imputation would distort spend-to-income ratios and introduce artificial centroids that don't reflect real customer behaviour.

02

Feature Selection

Ten numeric features describing customer spending and channel engagement were selected: Income, MntWines, MntFruits, MntMeatProducts, MntFishProducts, MntSweetProducts, MntGoldProds, NumWebPurchases, NumStorePurchases, Recency. Demographic columns and campaign-response flags were excluded to avoid label leakage and keep the model focused on spending behaviour.

03

Standard Scaling — Z-score Normalisation

All features were standardised using StandardScaler (µ = 0, σ = 1). K-Means relies on Euclidean distance — without scaling, features with large absolute ranges (Income in tens of thousands) would dominate features with small ranges (MntFruits in single digits), producing biased clusters. Scaling ensures every feature contributes equally to distance calculations.

04 — Model Training · K-Means (Scikit-learn)

Algorithm Configuration

  • Algorithm — sklearn.cluster.KMeans using Lloyd's algorithm.
  • n_clusters = 3 — Three segments represent low / mid / high-value customer archetypes, a common and interpretable starting point for retail segmentation.
  • n_init = 10 — The algorithm runs 10 times with different random centroid initialisations. Only the run with the lowest inertia is kept, reducing sensitivity to the starting point.
  • random_state = 42 — Fixed seed guarantees fully reproducible results on every run.
  • max_iter = 300 — Maximum allowed iterations per run (default). Convergence was reached well within this bound.
Inertia (within-cluster SSE): {{ "{:,.2f}".format(data.inertia) }} — total squared distance of each point from its assigned centroid. Lower values indicate more compact, tightly grouped clusters.

07 — Interpretation of Results

Budget Shoppers
Low-Value Segment

Customers with the lowest income and minimal product spending across all categories. They purchase infrequently and show low digital engagement. Targeted promotions, discount bundles, and loyalty rewards can improve their lifetime value at a low acquisition cost.

Mid-Range Buyers
Growth Segment

Middle-income customers with moderate spending, particularly on wines and meats. They show decent channel activity and represent the most actionable segment — upselling premium alternatives and personalised cross-selling can move them toward the high-value group.

Premium Customers
High-Value Segment

Highest-income customers with strong spending across all product categories, especially wines and meats. Highly engaged web and catalog shoppers. Retaining this segment through personalised campaigns and exclusive loyalty programmes is the top business priority.

Key Business Takeaways

  • Income is the dominant cluster separator — it accounts for most of the inter-cluster variance and drives all downstream spending patterns.
  • Wine & meat spend are the strongest per-category proxies for cluster membership — they scale almost linearly with income tier.
  • Recency does not strongly differentiate clusters — all three segments show similar days-since-last-purchase distributions, indicating it is an individual trait rather than a group characteristic.
  • Budget shoppers are the largest group (~49%); high-volume, low-margin promotions can raise their revenue contribution without heavy campaign investment.
  • Premium customers generate a disproportionate share of total revenue and warrant dedicated retention investment — losing them would have an outsized negative impact on sales.
